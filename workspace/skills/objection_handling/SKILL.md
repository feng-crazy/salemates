# Objection Handling

Transform objections into opportunities through systematic response strategies that address concerns while maintaining deal momentum.

## Overview

Objections are not rejections—they're requests for more information. Effective objection handling builds trust, demonstrates expertise, and often strengthens the relationship. The goal is to resolve concerns and advance the sale.

## Objection Types

### 1. Price Objections
Concerns about cost, budget, or ROI.

**Common Variations**:
- "It's too expensive"
- "We don't have the budget"
- "Your competitor is cheaper"
- "We need to think about it"

**Root Causes**:
- Value not established
- Budget cycle misalignment
- Competitor comparison
- No urgency

### 2. Value Objections
Concerns about whether the solution delivers sufficient benefit.

**Common Variations**:
- "I'm not sure we need this"
- "We're doing fine without it"
- "The ROI isn't clear"
- "I don't see how this helps us"

**Root Causes**:
- Incomplete discovery
- Misaligned messaging
- Features not connected to needs
- No pain established

### 3. Trust Objections
Concerns about company, product, or salesperson credibility.

**Common Variations**:
- "How do I know you'll deliver?"
- "You're a newer company"
- "We've never heard of you"
- "What if it doesn't work?"

**Root Causes**:
- Risk aversion
- Lack of social proof
- No established relationship
- Competitive incumbent

### 4. Authority Objections
Concerns about decision-making power or process.

**Common Variations**:
- "I need to check with my boss"
- "The committee needs to approve"
- "We need to get legal involved"
- "This isn't my decision to make"

**Root Causes**:
- Not the decision-maker
- Complex buying process
- Internal politics
- Hidden stakeholders

### 5. Timing Objections
Concerns about when to move forward.

**Common Variations**:
- "Now isn't a good time"
- "Call us back next quarter"
- "We're in the middle of something"
- "Let's revisit this later"

**Root Causes**:
- Low priority
- No compelling event
- Competing initiatives
- Genuine bad timing

### 6. Feature Objections
Concerns about specific functionality or capabilities.

**Common Variations**:
- "You don't have X feature"
- "Competitor Y has this"
- "We need it to do Z"
- "It won't work with our system"

**Root Causes**:
- Gaps in solution
- Custom requirements
- Legacy constraints
- Misunderstanding of capabilities

## Response Strategies

### The LAER Framework

**L - Listen**
- Let them finish completely
- Don't interrupt or anticipate
- Show you're engaged (nodding, "I understand")
- Take notes on specific concerns

**A - Acknowledge**
- Validate their concern
- Show empathy without agreeing
- Demonstrate understanding
- Avoid defensiveness

**E - Explore**
- Ask clarifying questions
- Uncover the real objection
- Understand the full context
- Quantify the concern

**R - Respond**
- Address the specific concern
- Provide evidence/examples
- Confirm resolution
- Advance to next step

### The Feel-Felt-Found Method

**Structure**:
1. "I understand how you **feel**..."
2. "Others have **felt** the same way..."
3. "What they **found** was..."

**Example - Price Objection**:

> "I understand how you feel about the investment—it's a significant decision. Other clients felt the same way initially. What they found after implementing was that the 3x productivity increase paid for the solution within 4 months, and they've seen continuous returns since. Would it help to see the ROI breakdown from a similar company in your industry?"

### The Isolate and Confirm Method

**When**: Multiple objections or unclear primary concern

**Process**:
1. "Besides [objection], is there anything else holding you back?"
2. "If we could resolve [objection], would you be ready to move forward?"
3. Address the isolated objection
4. Confirm resolution: "Does that address your concern?"

**Example**:

> Prospect: "It's too expensive, and I'm not sure it will work for us."
>
> Salesperson: "I understand. Besides the investment and implementation concerns, is there anything else that would prevent you from moving forward?"
>
> Prospect: "No, those are the main things."
>
> Salesperson: "If we could show you a clear ROI within 90 days and provide a success guarantee, would you be comfortable moving ahead?"
>
> Prospect: "Yes, I think so."
>
> Salesperson: "Great, let me show you how other companies in your situation achieved this..."

### The Boomerang Method

**When**: Objection can be turned into a reason to buy

**Structure**: Transform the objection into a benefit

**Example - "You're a small company"**:

> "You're right, we are a smaller company—and that's exactly why clients like working with us. You get direct access to our leadership team, faster response times, and we can customize solutions in ways larger vendors can't. You won't be just another account number to us. Would you prefer to be a priority client with us or a small fish in a big pond?"

## Example Dialogues

### Price Objection: "It's Too Expensive"

**Weak Response**:
> "Our prices are competitive in the market."

**Strong Response**:
> "I appreciate you being direct about the investment. To make sure I understand—what specifically feels expensive about it? [Explore]
>
> [After exploring] The companies that get the most value typically see a 340% ROI in year one. Let me show you how that breaks down for a company your size...
>
> [ROI breakdown] If this solution saves your team 15 hours per week at $75/hour, you're looking at $58,500 in annual savings—more than the full investment. What would it mean for your business if your team had those hours back?"

### Timing Objection: "Call Us Back Next Quarter"

**Weak Response**:
> "Okay, I'll follow up in a few months."

**Strong Response**:
> "I understand timing matters. Can you help me understand what's happening next quarter that makes it better timing? [Explore]
>
> [If genuine] Here's what I'm concerned about—by next quarter, you'll have lost another 3 months of potential ROI. What if we started with a smaller implementation now to begin seeing results, then expanded when you're ready? That way you don't lose more ground.
>
> [If uncertain] Many clients who said 'next quarter' found that when the quarter arrived, the same issues came up. What would need to change between now and then to make this a priority?"

### Competitor Objection: "We're Considering Competitor X"

**Weak Response**:
> "We're better than them because..."

**Strong Response**:
> "Competitor X is a solid company. Can you share what aspects of their solution appeal to you most? [Explore]
>
> [Acknowledge strengths, then differentiate] That makes sense. They do that well. Where clients who chose us over them found the real difference was in [key differentiator]. Let me share a specific example...
>
> [Transition] What matters most to you in this decision? I want to make sure we're comparing the things that will impact your day-to-day results."

### Trust Objection: "How Do I Know You'll Deliver?"

**Weak Response**:
> "We have a satisfaction guarantee."

**Strong Response**:
> "That's a fair question—you're making a significant investment and need confidence in the outcome. Let me address that a few ways:
>
> First, we've worked with [similar companies] and here's what they achieved: [specific results with permission to reference].
>
> Second, we offer a [specific guarantee] that protects your investment.
>
> Third, I can connect you with [customer name] at [company] who was in a similar situation to yours. Would it help to hear their experience directly?"

### Feature Objection: "You Don't Have X Feature"

**Weak Response**:
> "That feature is on our roadmap."

**Strong Response**:
> "I want to make sure I understand the need behind that feature request. Can you walk me through how you'd use that capability? [Explore]
>
> [After understanding] Here's how our current clients achieve a similar outcome: [alternative approach]. And actually, some find this way more flexible because [benefit].
>
> [If critical] I'll be direct—that feature is on our roadmap for Q3. If it's essential for your go-live, here are two options: we can prioritize it for an additional investment, or we can implement a temporary workaround until it's released. Which approach would work better for your situation?"

## Objection Prevention

### Discovery Phase
- Uncover concerns early: "What would prevent you from moving forward?"
- Address known objections proactively
- Build value before discussing price

### Presentation Phase
- Use customer language and examples
- Reference similar success stories
- Address likely concerns before they arise

### Closing Phase
- Test for objections: "What questions do you have?"
- Confirm decision criteria are met
- Summarize value and ROI

## Red Flag Objections

Some objections indicate fundamental misalignment:

| Objection | Likely Reality | Action |
|-----------|----------------|--------|
| "Send me some information" | Not interested / stalling | Push for meeting or disqualify |
| "We're all set" | No need identified | Re-qualify or move on |
| "Call me in 6 months" | Not a priority | Disqualify or long-term nurture |
| "I need to think about it" | Unresolved concern | Probe for real objection |

**Remember**: Every objection is a buying signal. If they weren't interested, they wouldn't object—they'd just say no. Handle objections with curiosity, not defensiveness.
